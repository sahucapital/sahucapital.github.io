Author's Note

A Note on Framing

This thesis is not a verdict on SpaceX as a company. It is an investigation into what the largest IPO in history may tell us about where we are in the capital cycle.

SpaceX is one of the most remarkable private companies ever built. Its achievements — reusable rockets, Starlink, commercial crew missions — are genuinely historic. This thesis does not challenge any of that.

What this thesis investigates is a narrower question that market participants consistently conflate: the quality of a business and the attractiveness of its IPO are not the same thing. History provides repeated, documented evidence of this distinction.

A great company can go public at the wrong valuation. A transformative technology can debut at a price that assumes a future that has not yet arrived. The story can be right while the entry price is wrong.

— Sahu Capital

The SpaceX IPO — the largest in recorded history at a $1.75 trillion valuation — provides a rare opportunity to examine capital cycle dynamics in real time. This report attempts to do exactly that, using publicly available data from SpaceX's S-1 filing, independent analyst valuations, and historical IPO precedents.

The Central Question

The implied valuation of $1.75 trillion would make SPCX the seventh-most-valuable publicly traded company in the United States at market open.

Morningstar's formal DCF valuation of $780 billion — published by analyst Nicolas Owens — represents the most rigorous independent estimate available. Owens's model values SpaceX's core launch and Starlink businesses at approximately $611 billion, with an additional $170 billion assigned to probability-weighted AI scenarios. His conclusion was unambiguous: "We think the company has been significantly overvalued and investors will have opportunities to buy the stock at more attractive levels after the IPO."

Damodaran's independent estimate sits around $1.3 trillion — still 26% below the IPO ask. The gap between independent fundamental valuations and the IPO target is not a rounding error. It is the central analytical question of this listing.

Inside the Financials

SpaceX is three companies operating under one ticker. Understanding which one you are actually buying matters enormously.

Starlink — The Profit Engine

The only profitable segment. 10.3 million subscribers as of Q1 2026. Accounts for ~69% of total revenue. Subscriber growth doubling year-over-year. However, average revenue per subscriber has declined 33% since 2023 as SpaceX pushes into lower-priced international and consumer markets. Volume is growing; pricing power is declining.
Aerospace — Cash Burning
$620M Q1

SpaceX launched 83% of all mass sent to orbit in 2025 — a genuine monopoly position. But the rocket business is currently loss-making. Over $15 billion invested in Starship development. The business requires Starship to reach commercial scale to justify the capital deployed. That milestone has not yet occurred.
xAI — The Biggest Drag
High burn
-$2.47B Q1 Loss
Acquired from Elon Musk in February 2026. R&D costs surged 300%+ after integration. xAI is projected to burn $10 billion in 2026 alone. Morningstar describes its economic moat as "indeterminate" and states: "We don't see Grok as one of the leading AI labs today." This segment represents the single largest risk to the investment thesis.

The financial picture that emerges from the S-1 is of a company where one profitable business (Starlink) is being asked to fund two capital-intensive, loss-making businesses (Aerospace and xAI) while being valued as though all three will simultaneously succeed at scale.

At $1.75 trillion, SpaceX trades at 94 times 2025 revenue. For context, this multiple assumes Starlink continues growing while reversing its ARPU decline, Starship achieves commercial scale on schedule, and xAI becomes competitive against OpenAI and Anthropic — all simultaneously. That is not a conservative assumption set.

Section 04

Capital Cycle Analysis

The most important question is not what SpaceX is worth. It is who is on the other side of the trade — and why now.

Capital cycle analysis asks a simple but often overlooked set of questions: Who is providing liquidity? Who is receiving it? And why is this happening now rather than earlier or later?

SpaceX has been private for over two decades. Employees, early investors, and private shareholders have accumulated positions over that entire period. The IPO provides the first opportunity for these holders to monetise their positions. This is not a criticism — it is simply a structural reality that public investors should understand before participating.

The SpaceX listing does not exist in isolation. Both OpenAI and Anthropic have filed confidentially with the SEC, with OpenAI targeting a September 2026 public debut and Anthropic closely behind. Investors who allocate to SPCX at $1.75 trillion will need to retain capacity for two additional large-scale AI company listings before year-end. The sequential demand on institutional capital is material and has not been widely discussed.

Section 06

Key Risks

Six specific risk factors that could pressure SPCX in the months following the IPO — regardless of SpaceX's long-term potential.

Lock-Up Expiry — December 2026
With insiders holding the vast majority of shares and a staged lock-up structure, December 2026 could see the largest single insider selling event in market history. Sentiment is typically hottest in the months post-IPO — exactly when the selling wall arrives.
xAI Capital Burn Accelerating
xAI is projected to burn $10 billion in 2026 alone. R&D costs surged 300%+ after integration. If xAI fails to close the gap with OpenAI and Anthropic, the segment may require continued capital injections with no clear path to profitability.
Starlink ARPU Compression
Average revenue per Starlink subscriber has declined 33% since 2023. The growth story increasingly depends on subscriber volume rather than pricing power. If subscriber growth slows while ARPU continues declining, the core profitability case weakens materially.
Sequential IPO Capital Competition
OpenAI targets September 2026. Anthropic follows. Both will compete for the same institutional capital pool that allocated to SPCX. Investors who deployed heavily into SpaceX may need to liquidate to participate in subsequent offerings.
Governance Concentration
Elon Musk controls approximately 85% of voting rights. Public shareholders have limited ability to influence corporate direction regardless of their economic stake. This governance structure concentrates both upside and risk in a single decision-maker.
Starship Execution Risk
Over $15 billion invested in Starship development — significantly exceeding original budgets. Commercial profitability depends on achieving a flight rate and reliability that has not yet been demonstrated at scale. A high-profile failure post-IPO would impact both sentiment and the launch business timeline.

Section 08

Final Conclusion

What the data says — stated as clearly and honestly as possible.

SpaceX is an extraordinary company. Its monopoly position in launch services, Starlink's genuine commercial success, and its long-term optionality in space infrastructure are real and valuable. This thesis does not dispute any of that.

What the data does suggest is that the $1.75 trillion IPO valuation — at 94 times revenue, with a GAAP net loss of $4.94 billion, carrying $30 billion in debt, with xAI burning $10 billion annually and Starlink ARPU declining 33% — prices in a version of SpaceX's future that has not yet materialised.

Morningstar's independent fair value estimate of $780 billion — produced by a credible analyst using a formal DCF model — implies that IPO-day buyers are paying approximately double fundamental value. Damodaran's estimate of approximately $1.3 trillion implies a 26% premium. These are not fringe views. They are disciplined analytical outputs from respected practitioners.

The structural conditions surrounding this IPO — unprecedented retail access, maximum AI enthusiasm, sequential capital competition from OpenAI and Anthropic, an insider lock-up wall arriving December 2026, and a tiny 3–7% free float creating artificial scarcity — are precisely the conditions that have historically preceded periods of post-IPO repricing.

The question this thesis posed at the outset was whether this represents an investment opportunity or a liquidity event. The honest answer, based on the available evidence, is that it appears to be primarily a liquidity event for existing holders — offered to the public at maximum enthusiasm, at maximum valuation, under maximum hype conditions.

SpaceX may ultimately prove worth $1.75 trillion or more. The long-term arc of the company could vindicate today's IPO buyers. But history's lesson is consistent: the best time to buy extraordinary companies is rarely their IPO — it is after the enthusiasm fades, the lockups expire, and fundamentals reassert themselves.

"A technology can revolutionise the world. Its stocks can still disappoint investors. The story can be right while the entry price is wrong. History has taught this lesson repeatedly. The question is whether investors choose to learn it again — or wait for a better entry."
