Author's Note

A Note on Perspective

This thesis is not written from a retail investor's perspective. It is an attempt to understand markets the way global capital does.

This thesis is not an argument that rising US bond yields are immediately bullish for Indian equities. The short-term mechanics of capital outflows, currency pressure, and valuation compression are real, documented, and painful for anyone positioned in Indian markets.

It is, however, an exploration of a second-order market effect — one that most participants miss entirely because they stop at the first-order conclusion.

The same macro forces that create panic, FII selling, and valuation destruction in the short term may also create one of the strongest long-term entry opportunities for patient global capital.

— Core Thesis · Sahu Capital

This paper studies US10Y dynamics, global capital allocation, FII behavior, currency depreciation, valuation compression, and emerging market cycles. The goal is not to predict exact bottoms. The goal is to understand how global capital behaves, how panic reprices assets, and how macro stress can create future opportunity.

Section 01

The Core Thesis

Why the dominant consensus about US bond yields may be missing a deeper, second-order truth about Indian equity markets.

The dominant consensus: Higher US Bond Yields → Stronger Dollar → EM Outflows → FII Selling → Weak INR → Bearish India. This thesis agrees with the short-term mechanics — but proposes a deeper possibility most participants miss entirely.

Second-order thinking asks: what does the FII outflow itself eventually produce? Three decades of market history answer this question with remarkable consistency.

Section 02

Understanding US10Y

Why a single number has the power to move trillions of dollars across the globe — including out of India.

The US 10-Year Treasury Yield is the global risk-free benchmark — the foundation upon which all asset valuations are built worldwide. When it rises sharply, global liquidity tightens, the dollar strengthens, and emerging markets face intense capital outflow pressure.

India does not compete with itself for global capital. India competes with the entire world. When US10Y offers 4.44%+ with zero credit risk, the hurdle rate for Indian equities rises dramatically — forcing mechanical portfolio rebalancing away from emerging markets.

India is not competing with itself. India is competing with the world. Every basis point rise in the US10Y raises the bar that Indian equities must clear to attract global capital.

Section 03

The FII Cost of Capital Framework

How institutional capital actually thinks — and why their decision-making is fundamentally different from retail participants.

Retail investors often misunderstand institutional investing. FIIs do not ask: "Will this stock go up?" They ask: "Is the expected return sufficient relative to global risk?"

Institutional capital is benchmark-sensitive. Rising US Treasury yields increase the opportunity cost of emerging market exposure. Simultaneously, a stronger dollar reduces translated returns from local-currency assets, forcing global portfolios to rebalance toward developed-market fixed income. This is not irrational panic — it is rational portfolio mathematics executed at scale.
