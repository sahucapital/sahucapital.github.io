Author's Note

A Note on Perspective

This thesis is not written from a retail investor's perspective. It is an attempt to understand markets the way global capital does.

This thesis is not an argument that rising US bond yields are immediately bullish for Indian equities. The short-term mechanics of capital outflows, currency pressure, and valuation compression are real, documented, and painful for anyone positioned in Indian markets.

It is, however, an exploration of a second-order market effect — one that most participants miss entirely because they stop at the first-order conclusion.

The same macro forces that create panic, FII selling, and valuation destruction in the short term may also create one of the strongest long-term entry opportunities for patient global capital.

— Core Thesis · Sahu Capital

This paper studies US10Y dynamics, global capital allocation, FII behavior, currency depreciation, valuation compression, and emerging market cycles. The goal is not to predict exact bottoms. The goal is to understand how global capital behaves, how panic reprices assets, and how macro stress can create future opportunity.

Section 03

The FII Cost of Capital Framework

How institutional capital actually thinks — and why their decision-making is fundamentally different from retail participants.

Retail investors often misunderstand institutional investing. FIIs do not ask: "Will this stock go up?" They ask: "Is the expected return sufficient relative to global risk?"

Institutional capital is benchmark-sensitive. Rising US Treasury yields increase the opportunity cost of emerging market exposure. Simultaneously, a stronger dollar reduces translated returns from local-currency assets, forcing global portfolios to rebalance toward developed-market fixed income. This is not irrational panic — it is rational portfolio mathematics executed at scale.

Section 04

The Currency Effect

Why INR depreciation simultaneously destroys short-term returns and creates long-term opportunity for patient global capital.

For FIIs, returns are measured in USD — not INR. This single fact changes everything. A stock can rise 8% in rupee terms while delivering a negative return to a dollar-based investor if INR depreciates 10%. This creates the initial wave of mechanical selling — driven by portfolio mathematics rather than Indian fundamentals.

Case Study 1 — 2008 Global Financial Crisis

The Nifty collapsed from 6,357 to 2,252 — a 65% decline in 9 months. FIIs sold $13.5 billion. INR weakened from ₹39 to ₹52. Yet 2009 saw a 76% recovery. Within 5 years: Lupin +700%, Sun Pharma +429%, TCS +306%, Asian Paints +304%.

Case Study 2 — 2013 Taper Tantrum

US10Y soared from 1.6% to nearly 3% in 3 months. INR lost 22% — hitting ₹68.85 lifetime low. India earned the "Fragile Five" label. Yet by December 2013, FIIs turned net buyers. Full recovery within 12 months. The panic that seemed systemic proved cyclical.

Case Study 3 — 2022 Fed Tightening Cycle

Fed Funds Rate peaked at 5.25–5.50%. FIIs sold ₹1.2 lakh crore — largest absolute selling episode. Yet by October 2025, FIIs returned with ₹24,000 crore in a single month. The pattern repeated for the third time in three decades.

History does not repeat itself exactly. But it rhymes with remarkable consistency. The question is never whether recovery will come. The question is whether you will be positioned for it.

— Sahu Capital
Section 06

The Second-Order Effect

Most participants see only what is happening. Few see what that event will eventually produce.

First-order thinking stops at: Higher US10Y = FII outflows = Bearish India. This is correct but incomplete. Second-order thinking asks: what does excessive selling itself produce? The answer, validated three times across three decades, is consistent — discounts attract patient capital, patient capital creates recoveries, recoveries create multibagger cycles.

Section 08

Risks to This Thesis

A serious thesis must define the conditions under which it fails. Intellectual honesty is the foundation of rigorous analysis.

Structural Growth Slowdown
If India's GDP growth decelerates significantly below 6% on a sustained basis, the fundamental earnings case weakens regardless of currency dynamics.
Banking System Stress
Emergence of significant NPA cycles or systemic credit stress could impair the financial system and undermine the quality argument for Indian large-cap equities.
Uncontrolled Currency Depreciation
If INR depreciation becomes structural and accelerating rather than cyclical, the discount window becomes a value trap rather than an opportunity.
Political or Regulatory Instability
Sudden policy reversals or governance concerns could permanently raise the risk premium FIIs demand from Indian assets.
Earnings Stagnation
Corporate earnings disappointing for multiple consecutive years would invalidate the fundamental valuation compression argument entirely.
Permanent Capital Reallocation
If global capital structurally shifts away from emerging markets, the historical recovery timeline extends significantly or breaks entirely.
Section 09

Final Conclusion

What three decades of market history, three major stress events, and one consistent pattern tell us about where opportunity forms.

The market consensus says: Higher US10Y = Bearish India. This thesis agrees with the short-term impact. But it has documented — across three separate historical crises spanning three decades — that the very forces causing short-term destruction have consistently created the foundations for long-term opportunity.

The 65% crash of 2008 created 700% multibaggers. The 22% INR collapse of 2013 preceded a full recovery within 12 months. The ₹1.2 lakh crore FII selling of 2022 was followed by ₹24,000 crore of FII buying in a single month by 2025.

Fear creates discounts. Discounts attract patient capital. Patient capital creates recoveries. And recoveries, in emerging markets with structural growth stories, create generational wealth.
